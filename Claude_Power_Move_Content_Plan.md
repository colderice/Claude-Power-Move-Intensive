## Content Pillars

### Pillar 1: The Operator Standard *(Authority)*

**What this pillar does:** Shows John doesn't just teach Claude — he runs his business on it. $32M in career sales, Verizon's AI Instructor, built a multi-million dollar operation from the ground up. This is demonstrated perspective, not credential-dropping. It quietly raises the bar for what's possible and positions the intensive as coming from someone operating at a different level entirely.

**Content Angles:**

- **"The Morning Difference"** — Walk through what a Monday looks like when Claude handles the brief. Not hypothetical. What actually runs. The contrast between that and a normal Monday spent writing emails does the selling.
  > *"Monday used to start with a pile. Now it starts with a brief. Claude pulls the priorities, drafts the outreach, preps the content queue. I'm reviewing, not generating. That one shift changed how the week runs."*

- **"What $32M Taught Me About Systems"** — The insight that volume-at-scale requires replication. AI isn't a trend response — it's the logical extension of everything that already worked.
  > *"You don't hit $32M by being the smartest person in the room. You hit it by building systems that work when you're not in the room. Claude is the next version of that system."*

- **"Why I Chose One Tool"** — The deliberate decision to strip everything back to Claude only. The complexity of multi-tool setups and why most people never get traction. Validates the intensive's design choice.
  > *"I've seen people spend a whole weekend setting up three AI tools and end up using none of them regularly. One tool, set up right, beats three tools set up halfway. Every time."*

- **"The Instructor Standard"** — What it means to teach at Verizon's Digital Ready program vs. teaching at a hotel ballroom. The difference between surface-level AI training and building something that runs.
  > *"Most AI training shows you what's possible. What I care about is what's operational. There's a difference. One gives you ideas. The other gives you time back."*

### Pillar 2: The Illusion Check *(Reality Exposure)*

**What this pillar does:** Targets the false comfort that's keeping people from registering. "I already use Claude" is not a system. "I'll figure it out eventually" is costing real hours right now. These posts create recognition first — then quiet discomfort. The tone is a straight-talking mentor who's seen this pattern a hundred times.

**Content Angles:**

- **"Casual Use Is Not a Strategy"** — The difference between prompting Claude when you're stuck vs. having Claude running workflows while you focus on what moves the needle.
  > *"Using Claude when you think of it is not the same as having Claude in your stack. One saves you twenty minutes. The other gives you back Tuesday."*

- **"The 5% Problem"** — Most people are getting 5-10% of what Claude can do. Not because they're not smart. Because no one showed them how to actually build with it.
  > *"I've watched smart business owners use Claude for two years and still be prompting it like it's a search engine. The tool was never the problem. The setup was."*

- **"Free Content Math"** — The actual cost of piecing this together alone. Not the dollar cost. The time cost. Six months of YouTube, Reddit threads, and trial-and-error vs. one Saturday with the system already built.
  > *"The free version of this education takes about six months and gets you halfway there. One day gets you the whole thing, configured to your actual business. You do that math."*

- **"The Competition Isn't Waiting"** — The coaches and consultants pulling ahead right now aren't more talented. They just moved earlier. The gap is compounding every month.
  > *"While you're still figuring out how to use AI, someone in your market already has it running. That's not a warning. It's a fact. The question is what you're doing about it."*

---

### Pillar 3: The Real Cost *(Trust Bridge)*

**What this pillar does:** Meets the audience where they actually are. Running a service business solo is hard. The overwhelm is real. The feeling of being behind is real. This pillar acknowledges all of it — then points toward motion without pushing. It removes the internal friction that logic can't touch.

**Content Angles:**

- **"I Know What Your Week Looks Like"** — Names the grind without dramatizing it. Emails that never end. Content that piles up. A business that depends entirely on your energy showing up.
  > *"You wake up and the first thing you do is answer something. Then you answer something else. By 10am you've put out fires but haven't moved anything forward. I know that week. I ran it for longer than I should have."*

- **"The Hustle Ceiling"** — The honest truth that effort alone has a cap. You can't outwork the math. At some point, more hours stops producing more results.
  > *"There's a ceiling you hit when everything depends on you showing up. I hit it. Most operators hit it. What you do at that ceiling is what separates the ones who scale from the ones who burn out."*

- **"What Keeps People from the Room"** — Name the real hesitation. Not the price. Not the date. The quiet belief that this might not work for someone like them.
  > *"I hear it all the time: 'I'm not really a tech person.' Good. This isn't a tech event. You bring what you know — your business, your clients, your problems. I bring the system. That's the deal."*

- **"The Permission You're Waiting For"** — For the person who keeps almost registering. Direct. No drama.
  > *"If you've looked at this page more than once, you already know the answer. The question is what you're waiting for. There's no version of this where figuring it out alone is faster."*

---

### Pillar 4: The Power Move Decision *(Conviction)*

**What this pillar does:** After the audience has had their worldview challenged, been seen, and watched the standard get raised — this pillar makes the decision feel inevitable. No hype. No countdown urgency. Quiet certainty that this is the obvious next move.

**Content Angles:**

- **"May 23 Is a Specific Day"** — Not a concept. A calendar date with a room, a location, a laptop requirement. Specificity is trust.
  > *"May 23. Masterly Studios, Atlanta. You bring a laptop and the problems that are eating your week. You leave with Claude handling them. That's what this day is."*

- **"The People Who Figure This Out First"** — Quiet conviction about market position. Not fear-based. Clarity about how first-mover advantage compounds in a service business.
  > *"In every industry, there's a window where the people who move first get an advantage that's almost impossible to close later. In your industry, that window is open right now. Not for long."*

- **"What $495 Is Really Buying"** — Reframe the price in terms of what it actually represents. Not a ticket. A configured system, 502 prompts, 5 workflow templates, and a full day with someone whose business runs on this.
  > *"The early bird price is $495. The value stack is $3,426. But the real thing you're buying is not the prompts or the templates. It's walking out with the setup done and never going back to doing it the hard way."*

- **"Two Kinds of May 24"** — The contrast without pressure. What May 24 looks like for someone who was in the room vs. someone who kept waiting.
  > *"There are two versions of May 24. One where you're taking what you built in the room and deploying it. And one where you're still meaning to figure this out. One of those versions is available to you right now."*

## Platform Notes

**Facebook / LinkedIn**: Full-length posts. Lead with the hook line — no preamble. Let it breathe. These audiences will read if you earn the first line.

**Instagram / X**: Strip it down to hook + closer. One idea, three to five lines. Pull the tension out of the long-form version and drop everything that's not doing work.

**Reels / Short Video** (highest-leverage formats in the final stretch): "The Morning Difference," "The 5% Problem," and "Two Kinds of May 24" all work as direct-to-camera 60-second videos. No script. Talk it out. These three will outperform any graphic post in the final two weeks.

## Why This Works for the Claude Power Move Intensive

The audience already knows AI exists and probably thinks they're keeping up. The belief gap isn't about awareness — it's about depth. They're using Claude at the surface level and telling themselves that's close enough. That comfortable lie is what this content strategy is built to break. Authority shows what operating at depth actually looks like. Reality Exposure names the gap between where they are and where they think they are. Trust Bridge removes the shame and makes motion feel possible. Conviction makes May 23 feel like the only move that makes sense. The early bird deadline on May 9 sits at the peak of the belief-challenge week — by the time the offer hits the table, the audience has already had their worldview interrupted three times. You don't pitch into a cold audience. You pitch into a shaken one.
